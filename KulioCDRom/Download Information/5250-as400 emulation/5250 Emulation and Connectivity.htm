5250 EMULATION & CONNECTIVITY

There was a time when all IBM midrange devices attached directly to their hosts, using twinax cable. Back then, 5250/SDLC protocol and the EBCDIC character set were the order of the day.

Then along came PCs -- with asynchronous communications and the ASCII character set -- and everything changed. PCs, of course, don't natively speak 5250/SDLC/EBCDIC, just like AS/400's don't natively speak ASCII/async. So in answer to this dilemma came 5250 emulation for PCs.

These days, nearly every AS/400 shop has to address the 5250 question, and the once-simple answers have been complicated by some powerful factors -- notably the Internet and the success of Microsoft's Windows family of operating systems. Today's 5250 emulation and connectivity solutions offer much in the way of choice and functionality, from low-end PC connectivity to advanced browser-based access. Here's a look at how the their features match up with your shop's needs.

FROM PAST TO PRESENT

Let's start with a little review. Several different solutions have been developed in support of 5250 emulation and communications -- first was the 5250 emulation card. Installed in a PC, the 5250 emulation card tricks the AS/400 into thinking the PC is a 5250-capable device by providing basic translation between the PC's ASCII/async world and the AS/400's 5250/SDLC/EBCDIC environment.

The next evolution of 5250 connectivity was IBM's PC Support, now Client Access/400. Beyond mere 5250 emulation, PC Support added features such as file transfer between the AS/400 and the PC, the ability to use PC printers as AS/400 printers, and shared folders that let you store PC data on the AS/400.

As IS Managers struggled with the world of PC Support, however, vendors were introducing other 5250 solutions, such as protocol converters, a purely hardware solution that translates the protocol of one computing device into the protocol of another. Whereas a 5250 emulation card can accommodate only one PC at a time, protocol converters can simultaneously accommodate multiple PCs or dumb terminals. Adding to their flexibility, protocol converters can be employed locally or remotely, and they can operate in a network.

The next step in the 5250 connectivity timeline is LANs. Ethernet and Token-Ring serve as the two most widely used LAN solutions in AS/400 installations, but these protocols are, of course, entirely different from either async or 5250/SDLC communications. Although we won't get into it in depth here, encapsulation is the technique that's used to handle this discrepancy.

LANs also brought network operating systems (NOSs), such as Novell's NetWare and Microsoft's Windows NT, to the AS/400 world. NOSs can add a lot of "chatter" to a LAN, so NOS vendors also created gateway products, such as NetWare for SAA and SNA Server, to perform the necessary translation between "LAN-speak" and 5250/SDLC. In addition, the gateways act as filters, routing only AS/400-related activity to the AS/400 (and leaving the chatter on the LAN).

The Internet has further complicated the world of 5250 emulation, as the Internet's native protocol is TCP/IP. Many of today's AS/400 installations run TCP/IP -- even in combination with Ethernet or Token-Ring. And many TCP/IP packages on the market actually simplify 5250 communications through a utility commonly known as TN5250. The TN refers to Telnet, a TCP/IP utility that lets one computer sign on to another -- so TN5250 allows, for example, a PC to sign on to an AS/400 and appear to the AS/400 as a 5250 terminal.

THE HARD(WARE) FACTS

Java and Windows technology are decidedly changing the face of PC-to-AS/400 5250 connectivity. But what about the hardware? After all, it was the introduction of PCs that started this whole process. There are, in fact, some exciting developments in client-to-AS/400 hardware. Of particular interest is the area of Network Computers (NCs, sometimes called network stations or thin clients). NCs have many benefits. Key among them:

  • Many NCs have only one moving part -- the On/Off switch. No moving parts means less hardware (e.g., disk drives) to break.
  • Because software is installed on a server and propagated automatically to the NCs as users sign on, software and user-profile administrative tasks all take place at a central site -- making admin itself less tasking.
  • NCs take up much less desktop space than PC cabinets. Many NCs are the size (and weight) of a hardback novel.
  • NCs are much less expensive to buy and administrate than PCs.
  • NCs (typically) are based on open architectures -- not dependent on any specific vendor's CPU chip or operating system.

NCs are not a flash in the pan. As time goes on, they'll find homes in more and more AS/400 installations. They can accomplish all of the 5250 and applications connectivity that PCs can, but at lower costs. And when combined with the power and openness of Java, NCs spell the future of AS/400 5250 connectivity.

But the Internet has even more 5250-related tricks up its sleeve in the form of browsers and Java.

Clearly browsers, such as Microsoft's Internet Explorer or Netscape's Navigator, have become the standard interface to the Internet, including access through network computers (NCs). Browsers can also use applications such as TN5250 to access AS/400 data and applications.

And in the latest developments, Java programmers have responded to the needs of 5250 emulation: Several vendors now have Java-based 5250 emulation products. In fact, Java 5250 emulation could be the final word on 5250 emulation, because every device in the world capable of supporting the Java Virtual Machine (JVM) can run Java-based 5250 emulation.

BUT WHAT DO YOU NEED?

The world of 5250 emulation has seen many twists and turns, and today, all of these solutions are still in use to one degree or another. In fact, 5250 connectivity -- in any form or by any means -- may be more important now than at any time in the past, as the several flavors of Windows and the growth of the Internet have added complexity to selecting a 5250 solution. Today's AS/400 managers must -- in most cases -- factor in Windows operability and browser compatibility along with "simple" 5250 networking.

For illustration's sake, We'll presume that you fall into one of two categories:

  • Basic 5250 users -- AS/400 shops that simply want to eliminate their in-place 5250 terminals and use PCs to connect to the AS/400, or are using PCs and a 5250 connectivity solution but want to update or replace their current 5250 technology with one of the more robust solutions available today. As a rule-of-thumb, the AS/400 installations that fall into this category are not much interested in deploying advanced 5250 solutions such as Java-based 5250 emulation, or the Internet -- at least not today.
  • Advanced 5250 users -- AS/400 installations that may have an existing 5250 connectivity installation in place or are still using dumb terminals, but want to replace them with a leading-edge solution. The focus is on the very latest technology in AS/400-to-PC connectivity. Furthermore, these installations may have an interest in intranets or the Internet. In short, this group has the same 5250 connectivity needs as the basic users, and then some.

Of course, both groups share certain underlying requirements regarding 5250 connectivity:

  • PC-to-AS/400 5250 emulation
  • File and database serving
  • Printer-emulation capability
  • Windows 3.x, 95, and/or NT connectivity

We'll discuss more-advanced 5250 requirements shortly.

HOW A 5250 EMULATION SOLUTION IS BUILT TODAY

Let's look at several functions of today's 5250 emulation products and see how they relate to your shop's needs.

PC-to-AS/400 5250 Emulation

Function: Lets PCs emulate 5250 terminals

Minimum Requirements: Single-session emulation of a 5250 screen with attendant functionality (e.g., command keys, attention keys, keyboard mapping).

What to Look For: The session capabilities of today's 5250 emulators have come a long way from the days of simple green-screen mirroring. Today, a good 5250 emulator should have the capability for:

  • Color screens -- the ability to use colors beyond "IBM green" on 5250 panels, menus, and so on. Industry research has shown that color not only enhances the experience of working with a PC display, it also improves user productivity.
  • A graphical environment for working with existing AS/400 applications
  • Display and printer sessions
  • Cut and paste operations
  • Support for 5250 emulation on PCs running only DOS
  • Ability to give authorized users access to the AS/400 through OS/400 security
  • Support for multiple, simultaneous AS/400 sessions
  • Remote configuration and troubleshooting capabilities

Especially important to advanced installations is the ability to install a 5250 solution only once and have that solution available to all users as soon as they sign on. Most 5250 emulators in this classification are Java based (more details below).

PC-to-AS/400 5250 File and Database Serving

Function: File serving lets you store PC files on the AS/400 and access them from any PC in an attached network. Database serving provides access to all AS/400 database information (similar to Structured Query Language, or SQL).

Minimum Requirements: Ability to store PC data and applications on an AS/400 system and access these files as if they were on a PC hard disk or a PC server

What to Look For:

  • Concurrent access (for multiple users) to the same data, using byte-level locking
  • Ability to use OS/400 security to give authorized users access to the AS/400 data
  • Bi-directional file transfer
  • File Transfer Protocol (FTP) support (for advanced users)
  • Binary file transfer capability (binary file transfers are much faster than other, traditional file transfers)
  • Window-oriented drag-and-drop file transfer
  • ODBC (Open Database Connectivity)

Printer Emulation

Function: Lets a PC access and use an attached PC printer as if it were an AS/400 locally attached printer.

Minimum Requirements: Some 5250 emulators, although labeled as plug-and-play, actually require quite a bit of configuration and offer little or no help. Real plug-and-play emulation cards (and associated software) make it easy to install them -- using, for example, Wizards to help guide you through the installation and set-up process.

What to Look For: Basic installations require emulation of common AS/400 printers such as IBM's 3812, 5219, 4214, 4210, 5225, 5224, and 5256 offerings. Advanced installations will want to make sure that, in addition to IBM printer emulation, their 5250 product contains full LPR/LPD support.

As for additional features, some of today's 5250 printer emulation offerings include "smart printing," which means the printer emulator has complete Windows-like control of printing functions such as landscape/portrait printing or number of copies to be printed. Consider it a major "plus" if the printer/emulator uses "transmit one, print many" technology. This technique lets you transmit the actual file across your network only once and then print multiple copies from that file.

A FEW WORDS ABOUT CLIENT ACCESS

For many AS/400 professionals, the lightning rod around which all discussion of 5250-related technology occurs is IBM's Client Access/400. But with the advancements occurring all over the 5250 market, most shops -- especially advanced ones -- are hard-put to find a good reason to deploy a new installation of IBM's Client Access/400 product.

Why? Because there are other packages available in the marketplace that, for all intents and purposes, perform the tasks of Client Access, but at less cost and, in many cases, with better
(or simpler, or more robust) functionality.

How can this be true? The answer is, simply, market dynamics. The dynamics of the computing landscape have conspired to relegate Client Access to the "remember when" category of AS/400 products.

When IBM decided to put full TCP/IP support onto the AS/400 (as of V3R1), it did so knowing that TCP/IP (and its associated utilities, such as Telnet 5250) might well ring the death knell for Client Access.

First, TCP/IP is a more mature technology than Client Access, having been in constant use since the 1960s. Also, the longevity of TCP/IP means that there are scores -- if not hundreds -- of companies in the world producing innovations in TCP/IP utilities, add-ons, and so on. Few technology vendors, on the other hand, spend precious R&D dollars on Client Access add-ons. In addition, there are tens of thousands of TCP/IP experts -- far more than Client Access yields. In fact, to the thousands of TCP/IP experts in the world (think about the sheer numbers of professionals involved in TCP/IP-related activities on the Internet alone) the AS/400 is just another node on a TCP/IP network.

The wide body of available TCP/IP expertise means that its support costs can be -- and often are -- lower than Client Access. The Internet, itself based on TCP/IP, is chock-full of Web sites and other repositories of knowledge about TCP/IP-related tools, tips, and techniques for getting the most out of the protocol. And even mainstream book stores such as Borders, Barnes and Nobel -- and of course Amazon.com -- have hundreds of books on understanding, installing, and tweaking TCP/IP. Obviously, Client Access doesn't enjoy nearly as much fame and widespread appeal.

Oh ­ and there's one more detail in favor of TCP/IP over Client Access: IBM includes TCP/IP at no cost with OS/400.

So who should be using Client Access? There are two classes of AS/400 installations that can be advised to go the Client Access route: those that have a huge (and expensive) investment in Client Access and can't currently afford or justify the move off of Client Access and onto other solutions; and installations that have relatively basic 5250 emulation needs (those defined as "basic 5250 users" in the accompanying article).

Microsoft/Windows Support

Function: Allows for the use of Microsoft products, such as Windows and SNA Server, in conjunction with 5250 emulation sessions. Also supports Windows programming techniques, as detailed below.

Minimum Requirements: A 5250 client for basic installations should at least support Windows 3.x, Windows for Workgroups, or Windows 95. Advanced installations should look for emulation products that support Windows 95, Windows NT, and Microsoft's SNA Server. NT support is especially important due to that system's increasing deployment in AS/400 installations and its availability on the AS/400 as of OS/400 V4R2. SNA Server support is also mandatory for advanced installations because -- to oversimplify things -- SNA Server reduces the configuration complexity for Windows-AS/400 networks, and it reduces nodal overhead.

What to Look For: Both basic and advanced 5250 shops should make sure that the emulation solution they choose supports the version of Windows (16- or 32-bit) in use at their AS/400 installations. Also, Windows-oriented programming techniques -- such as hot spots, list boxes, and so on -- are important considerations for advanced installations.

PC-to-AS/400 Network Connectivity

Function: Lets PCs use a variety of means to physically or logically attach to the AS/400.

Minimum Requirements: Because of the widespread popularity and acceptance of Windows and Windows applications that need to logically connect to the AS/400 for data, all emulation solutions, for either basic or advanced installations, must have support for ODBC.

What to Look For: With the complex nature of today's AS/400 environments, connectivity is a tricky thing. However, when shopping for a 5250 emulation solution, basic users should look for a 5250 emulation solution vendor whose products can attach to, or access, an AS/400 via twinax, async, Token-Ring, or Ethernet. Advanced users will also want connectivity via TCP/IP.

Both groups should also look for products that include a Windows-capable APPC router. Although there are some leading-edge products on the market today that have dispensed with the need for a separate router, routing itself will continue to be an issue in almost all basic 5250 installations as well as many (if not most) advanced shops. Only the most progressive, leading-edge AS/400 shops -- those committed, for example, to advanced thin-client technology -- should consider a 5250 solution that does not use a router.

On another front, any installation that's considering a move to TCP/IP as a local networking alternative -- or an advanced shop that's looking to deploy an intranet or extranet, or to use the Internet -- must ensure that its 5250 product includes support not only for basic TCP/IP and FTP, but also LPR/LPD, e-mail, VB scripting, and a Web browser (preferably Netscape's Navigator or Microsoft's Internet Explorer, which cover most of the browser market).

Advanced installations that use either Microsoft Windows or Novell NetWare will need emulation solutions that support NetWare for SAA and/or SNA Server, respectively.

Plug-and-Play Capability

Function: Simplifies the installation process.

Minimum Requirements: The phrase "plug and play" is often thrown about loosely, and you can't rely on it to mean what it should. When it comes to 5250 emulation, however, it's important to know that you'll be able to install and set up the product smoothly.

What to Look For: In a word, Wizards -- or at least a well-documented installation process and on-line Help guides.

Many 5250 emulation vendors offer demo versions of their hardware (and software) for evaluation purposes. Be sure to take advantage of these demonstration copies -- but be sure to install the demo on a PC exactly like those your users are employing. Many IS professionals install demo products on their own souped-up PCs and are later surprised to find the products don't perform the same way when deployed on less powerful machines.

Tech Support

Function: Allows for quick answers to 5250 emulation-related questions and problems

Minimum Requirements: Although this "feature" may seem obvious, tech support and help support have become critical issues in today's (primarily networked) AS/400 environments. This is especially true in advanced installations, which may be using 5250 emulation solutions that haven't been subjected to years of day-in, day-out use by thousands of AS/400 shops. Any vendor whose products you are considering should have a toll-free phone number that connects you directly to its customer support department.

What to Look For: Beyond toll-free support, consider a well-documented Web site with e-mail access, FAQs, and a message board to be a plus. Make sure, however, that a vendor's Web site contains technical documentation and not just marketing-oriented information. In today's world, there is no excuse for a vendor to not have every scrap of technical and anecdotal data (read: real-world problems from actual users) about its products on the Web.

THE NEXT GENERATION

Now let's move away from the basics and imagine a world in which:

  • You can distribute OS/400 spool files automatically, without using SNADS or other so-called "native" AS/400 tools -- and make sure that the right spool file gets to the right user and only that user.
  • Installing client software requires only one installation, which will automatically propagate to every desktop in your network -- without any human involvement beyond users signing on to their desktop PCs or NCs.
  • Remote users can connect to your AS/400 without needing any pre-installed software on their PCs and can execute AS/400 tasks with amazingly fast (in some cases, sub-second) response times -- over the Internet.
  • File transfers from your AS/400 to a desktop PC require only a drag-and-drop action on a fully GUI'd view of both your AS/400 and PC files.

Those functions, and other sophisticated solutions like them, are available today through leading-edge 5250 emulation products that work over the Web.

The enormously important explosion of Internet technology into everyday life has heralded many of the technologies detailed above. Key among these Internet-related technologies are Java, push technology, and the thin client or NC.

In fact, the future of 5250 connectivity might be summed up in one word: Java. Companies such as ADVANCED BusinessLink, Better On-line Solutions, Core Technology Corporation, Farabi Technology, OpenConnect Systems, and others, are using Java to deliver new functions and new performance levels in 5250 clients on the Web. Some of these features include:

  • Java-based compression routines that reduce response times across the Internet
  • Enhanced security with Java-written SSL (and other) encryption routines
  • Push technology, using the AS/400 as a server
  • Easier maintenance and implementation through centralized Internet-server- or LAN-server-accessed Java applets instead of installations on every desktop

In the earliest stages of 5250 emulation, having a 5250 session on your screen was the best you could hope for. But now we have technologies to bring LAN access, remote access, and even Internet access -- from anywhere in the world -- to deliver highly sophisticated functions such as database queries, spool management and distribution, graphical interfaces with cut-and-paste capabilities, secure access to multiple servers, and the many advanced features that are made available through Java.

Early 5250 emulation products led the way for the AS/400 to become connected to the departmental desktop world. Today, you have a world of choices to meet your 5250 emulation and connectivity needs.
