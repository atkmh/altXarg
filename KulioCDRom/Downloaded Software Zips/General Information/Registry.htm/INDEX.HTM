by Matthew Lake
(3/27/97)

t's been almost two years since Windows 95 made its heavily hyped debut, so you've had plenty of time to learn your way around the operating system. By now, your Start menu is a masterly work of organization. And your Windows desktop is tidier than the one your monitor is sitting on. You think you're pretty handy with Shortcuts. And maybe, just maybe, your network administrator secretly comes to you for Win 95 advice.

Don't get too cocky, though. While you've been pressing Ctrl-Esc to bring up your Start menu, others have been skulking around in Registry settings, getting cryptic key code stuck under their fingernails, and really making a difference in how Windows 95 works.

    Legions of power users have grown up trespassing in off-limits Windows 95 space. As they've made changes that their pastor, principal, or parole officer wouldn't approve of, they've uncovered a deep truth. Hidden beneath its veneer, where angels fear to tread, are ways to improve Windows 95...or at least make it look a little better.

    These Win 95 hackers employ fistfuls of techniques that may be subversive, that may not be Microsoft-approved, that may even invalidate tech support policies. But for the elite group of people
    willing to plunge into the depths of their operating system, the guilty pleasures and the interesting results far outweigh the risks and hassles. And if you pay close attention to CNET's instructions, you too can join the ranks of Win 95 propeller heads.

    Of course, if cracking Windows 95 were completely safe, the pleasure in doing it would diminish considerably. That's why it's a good idea to be careful with these tips--and to be sure that you read and follow CNET's "seven commandments of Registry editing." But once you've mastered our ten secret techniques, you'll be a certified Windows 95 guru, with plenty of time to play Freecell while the rest of the class catches up.

Matthew Lake has written two serious books about Windows--Breaking into Windows (ZD Press, 1993) and Windows 95: Making It Work for You (ZD Press, 1995)--and has coauthored and contributed to more than a dozen books about technology. He writes the Corporate PC column for Computer Currents.
